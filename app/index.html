Enid Blyton

The Magic Faraway Tree published in 1951 was one of the first novels I read. I particular liked this one because it made me imagine the existence of alternate worlds.

Imagination can lead to discovery and that is exactly what Enid relied on. Every page in her book was full of adventure and I still enjoy going back and reading through them, reminiscing my beautiful childhood moments. Enid indeed mastered childrens literature, no doubt she was the best seller since 1930. She is even remembered today as the creator of Noddy, the writer of the famous series Secret Seven, Famous Five and Adventures of the Wishing Chair.

The Magic Faraway Tree was a turning point for me. I believed someday I would create something as magical as this and sway people with my creation.

Hot Wheels - Next Gen

I tried to display my texturing skills through this project. My idea was to create a hot wheels car with a futuristic skin.

I took my inspiration from a species called Umbonia Spinosa. Each object in the scene was UV mapped. PSD networks were created to apply texture and bump maps accordingly using Photoshop.

Poverty

My interest lies in painting and portraying the real world problems. During my PBD study at Simon Fraser University, I was encouraged to watch numerous Ted Talks by Gary Haugen, Hans and Ola Rosling, and submit reports on my understanding of the topic. Few years ago, I was browsing through my collection of paintings that depicted my view on this very topic. It was my interpretation of how the world looked like from afar. Two worlds aware of each other’s existence live together in utmost distress.

I modeled the design from my concept art. I used paint effects to create some of the environment, converted them into polygon and rendered it using mental ray. Mountains were defined through sculpting tools. Using bifrost for rendering this large scale project became difficult even after caching. So, I used a hardware friendly technique and used ocean shader to present the same. The cracking of the glass represents my thoughts of poverty being an illusion.

Silly Symphony (1936)

In the 90s, the television set became affordable enough for my family to own. In between schoolwork, playtime and naptime, I was allowed to watch TV for about 3 hours a day. We had very few channels to browse through and had a schedule to follow. One of my favorites was the 5 o’clock show - “Silly Symphonies”. It was a platform for Walt Disney Productions to provide entertainment, and at the same time experiment with the art of animation. Wilfred Jackson’s “The Grasshopper and the Ants”, a story from Aesop’s fables, featured in the series. The episodes not only had funny moments, but also helped to mold kids’ behaviors by teaching them right from wrong. I have created a representation of the Grasshopper by modeling it in the 3D world using Autodesk Maya 2016 and performed the whole body rigging to animate it when needed.

Face Morphing

The idea for the project struck when I was sent a credit card statement via mail. It had an ad of an infant missing from 1989. 26 years later, people still find the motivation to look for their kids without knowing how they may look. Morphing is a special effect in motion pictures and animations that changes (or morphs) one image or shape into another through a seamless transition. I created the morphing process through Adobe Photoshop to assist in finding the missing person and help the grieving parents find peace when there is little hope of reconciliation. The technology can also help in capturing criminals who have been evading the law for a long period of time by generating variations of what the culprit will look under disguise. This will give rise to new ways to crack cold cases.
